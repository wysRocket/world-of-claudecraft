Professions System

High-level design for gathering and crafting in World of ClaudeCraft. It deliberately deviates from classic WoW: everyone gathers everything, and a crafting specialty emerges from what you make rather than from a class you pick at the start.

How it works at a glance

The system has two activities. Read these first, because everything below builds on them.

  • Gathering collects raw materials from the world (ore, hide, herbs, monster parts). Everyone can do all of it, so it is not where you specialize.
  • Production turns those materials into finished items (armor, weapons, potions, enchants). This is where you specialize, and where most of this document lives.

The whole design serves one aim: a specialty should emerge from playing, and a specialist should be clearly better than a generalist. You become "a smith" by making swords and armor, not by choosing a class.

The two activities reach that aim with opposite math, named here in plain words and detailed later:

  • Production is conserved. At any moment only one specialty is active: a small cluster of adjacent crafts, empowered to the top tiers while the rest sit on the common floor. You can learn as many crafts as you like over time and never lose what you learned, but you can keep only one specialty empowered at once, and switching which one is active has a real cost. That cost is exactly what makes a specialist worth more than a dabbler.
  • Gathering is additive. Choosing to harvest one thing better never weakens your ability to harvest anything else. Breadth is free.

The production wheel

Production is a ring of ten crafts. You get better at a craft by making things with it, and because production is conserved (above), your active specialty sits in a small region of the ring rather than spread evenly across it. Two facts follow from the ring's geometry, and the rest of this section unpacks them:

  • Adjacent crafts are partners. Neighbours on the ring go together, so a specialist ends up strong in a pair of adjacent crafts.
  • Opposite crafts are complements. The craft directly across the ring tends to make the very sub-component your main craft consumes, which matters once we reach hobbies.

Two words of vocabulary, used consistently from here on: a craft is one of the ten production skills (its skill-form name labels the wheel, for example Tailoring); a practitioner is a person who works it (for example a Tailor). The ten crafts are Armorcrafting, Weaponcrafting, Tailoring, Leatherworking, Alchemy, Cooking, Inscription, Enchanting, Engineering, and Jewelcrafting.

Conserved mass: one active specialty and the at-most-two rule

Skill is learned additively: every craft has a level that only ever grows as you work it, and nothing you learn is ever taken away. What is conserved is not the skill total but your active specialty. At any moment only one cluster of the ring is empowered to the top tiers; every other craft sits on the common floor regardless of what you have dabbled in. You can broaden your knowledge as much as you like over a character's life, but you can keep only one specialty live at a time.

  • The active specialty is at most two adjacent crafts empowered to their ceiling (plus a single opposite hobby up to rare, detailed under Archetypes below). That cap is the heart of the design: it is what a specialist has and a dabbler does not.
  • The craft directly opposite your majors is not empowered, which is what stops anyone wielding both sides of the ring at once. The one carve-out is the hobby: the single opposite craft you take stays empowered up to the rare tier, while the rest of the opposite side stays at the floor.
  • There is no skill loss and no respec fee. You switch which specialty is active through attunement (see How it's experienced), not by grinding the old one away. Your previous specialty's skill stays on the books, dormant at the floor, ready the moment you attune back.
  • This pressure only applies from the uncommon tier upward; common-tier crafting is a free floor that never competes (see Tiered mastery, next).

Figure 2. Specialist vs generalist

The specialist (a Smith) has attuned to one archetype: two adjacent crafts empowered to the top, a little in the flanks, the opposite side at the floor. The generalist never attuned and stays competent everywhere, master of nothing. Both may have learned just as widely; only the specialist has a live specialty.

Tiered mastery and the common-tier floor

Skill in each craft climbs through the game's item rarity tiers (common, uncommon, rare, epic, legendary), but only up to the ceiling of your current tier: crafting raises a craft to the top of the tier it is at, and a per-tier quest is what unlocks the next tier's recipes and its higher ceiling. So the wheel is really a ladder of quest-gated tiers, not one smooth bar.

  • Common is a free floor. Any craft's common recipes can be made by anyone at no cost to specialization, so a master alchemist can still bang out a basic (common) sword without affecting their alchemy. The at-most-two cap only applies at uncommon and above.
  • At-tier crafting is what advances you. Crafting below your current capability gives diminishing returns, and a craft two tiers under your capability gives nothing. A rare-capable alchemist gains zero from common potions; they have to work at their tier to progress.
  • Above the ceiling, the xp is lost. If you craft while already at the top of your current tier, the excess skill is not banked; it is simply lost until you unlock the next tier by completing that craft's tier quest. This holds at every boundary, including common to uncommon.
  • The ceiling has two identical causes. A craft can be capped either because it is not in your active archetype (attunement has not empowered it), or just because you have not done its next tier quest yet. Both behave the same way: skill to the cap, lose the rest. The tier-quest ladder is how any craft climbs, and attunement is which crafts you are allowed to climb to the top.
  • Only uncommon-and-up commits you. Pushing a craft to uncommon or higher is what builds toward an active specialty; dabbling at common across many crafts never commits you to anything.

Figure 3. Tiered reach: the common floor and the specialization window

The rings are the rarity tiers. The inner teal ring is the common-tier floor, which every craft reaches for free. Above common, your reachable tier narrows to a window: a Smith's two adjacent crafts climb to legendary, their flanks reach about rare, and the opposite side stays at the common floor while this specialty is active.

Worked example. A new player crafts 20 healing potions and tops out common-tier alchemy, then completes its tier quest to become uncommon-capable. Further common potions now give less, and once a later tier quest makes them rare-capable, common potions give zero, so progress means working at their tier. They then craft 20 basic (common) swords and pick up weaponcrafting too; nothing they learned in alchemy is lost. As they push weapons into uncommon and beyond, they are building a second specialty. They can keep only one specialty active at a time, so when they attune as a weaponcrafter their alchemy knowledge stays on the books, dormant at the floor, until they attune back.

Archetypes: your two majors and a hobby

Because the active specialty empowers only two adjacent crafts, every specialist ends up defined by a pair of adjacent crafts, their two majors. Each of the ten adjacent pairs around the ring is a recognizable identity, so there are ten named archetypes (Smith, Outfitter, Apothecary, Arcanist, and so on).

  • On top of the two majors you also get a hobby: one craft from the pair sitting directly opposite your majors, worked up to the rare tier. This is the carve-out from the at-most-two rule: the opposite side is otherwise unempowered, but the single opposite craft you take as a hobby is empowered up to rare. Like the majors, the hobby is part of your attunement: it emerges from what you craft, and you switch it to the other opposite when you re-attune, not by grinding the first away. The rare cap is what keeps a dabbler from ever matching a real specialist, and keeps high-tier crafting interdependent.
  • The opposite-as-hobby doubles as a self-supply line, because opposite crafts are complements: a Smith (Armorcrafting plus Weaponcrafting) takes Tailoring or Leatherworking as a hobby, exactly the cloth padding and leather straps a full set of gear needs.

You do not pick any of this from a menu; it emerges from what you craft (explained in How it's experienced, below). The explorer below lets you tour the ten outcomes: click an archetype on the ring to see its majors fill to legendary and its hobby cap at rare.

How it's experienced: emergence, attunement, and switching

You never pick an archetype from a menu. You craft freely, your crafting trends toward an adjacent pair, and the game offers to name and attune you to it. The archetype catalog above is a designer's view of the ten outcomes, not an in-game list: "Smith" is a title the game grants you for making swords and armor, not a class you chose.

The system reveals itself in steps, each tied to the mechanic that triggers it:

  • Common tier: craft anything with no pressure (common is the free floor). The skill radar is visible but flat.
  • Each tier is quest-gated: crafting raises a craft to the top of its current tier, and a tier quest unlocks the next tier's recipes and ceiling. The first of these (common to uncommon) is the moment specialties start to matter, and a one-time tutorial panel explains the at-most-two rule and the radar exactly when the mechanic first bites. So attunement is a ladder, not a single event: each step deeper into an archetype is its own tier quest.
  • Two adjacent crafts climb into rare (a specialty has unmistakably emerged): the tier quest at this point also grants the title, empowers your two majors, and reveals your hobby.

Switching specialties is done by attunement, not by grinding the old one away. Because you learn additively, nothing you have earned is ever lost; instead, your active archetype is set by a quest, which keeps the switch cost diegetic and out of the live economy's moving prices, and gives the game a natural place to spell out exactly what attuning will change on your character.

  • The first time into any given archetype, whether or not you have ever held one, is a lore quest: an earned, satisfying first step into that identity rather than a tax.
  • Returning to a specialty you previously held is a distinct repeatable quest where you make amends for your lack of loyalty. Its cost escalates with how many times you have done it, and a quest can carry anything: a time delay, a coin or material cost, more lore. Your old skill is untouched, so the cost is the quest, never a re-grind.

Combo-specific recipes (archetype-exclusive)

Some recipes require both crafts of a specific adjacent pair, so they belong to exactly one archetype, a signature line nobody else can make. A baited bear trap (Trapper: Leatherworking plus Cooking) is unavailable to an Outfitter (who has the leather but not the cooking) or an Apothecary (who has the cooking but not the leather).

  • Exclusivity scales with tier, for free. Only your active archetype has both crafts of an adjacent pair empowered, so a high-tier combo (a rare or epic reinforced trap) is exclusive to whoever is attuned to that pair. A crafter mid-migration, holding a third adjacent craft at a reduced tier, might make the basic version, so low-tier combos are semi-open and top-tier combos are locked.
  • Respects the common floor. Signature combos live at uncommon and above, so they never break "anyone can craft common basics".
  • The hobby cannot cheat it. Your hobby is the opposite craft (capped at rare, not adjacent to either major), so it never unlocks a combo recipe.
  • Deepens the economy. Each archetype is the sole high-tier source of its line, exactly the interdependence the design is chasing.

Legibility: nudges while crafting, the full picture at attunement

Legibility rests on one rule: never punish a craft, always explain a switch. Because crafting only ever adds skill, the act of crafting never costs you another craft and never needs a blocking warning. Legibility instead lives in two calmer places: gentle nudges while you craft, and the full explanation at the moment you actually change something, the attunement quest.

While crafting, the game informs, it does not warn. Common-tier crafting is silent. Crafting toward an adjacent pair surfaces an informational nudge ("you are trending toward Cogsmith; see a trainer to attune"), and nothing you craft ever lowers another skill, so there is no Cancel / Ignore dialog to dismiss.

At attunement, the quest spells out the whole picture before you commit: which crafts become empowered, what your title becomes, that any previously active specialty will go dormant (not lost) until you attune back, and, for a return, the escalating make-amends cost. This is the one place the system asks you to confirm a real change, and it does so in the quest, not a popup.

The two tricky edge cases resolve the same calm way:

  • Two non-adjacent high crafts (Inscription to rare, then Leatherworking to rare): nothing fails and nothing drops. You simply have no adjacent pair to attune, so you remain (or attune as) a Jack of All Trades (below), with a hint on how an adjacent pair would form an archetype.
  • Switching your hobby: take the other opposite craft as part of re-attuning. The old hobby goes dormant and the new one is empowered up to rare; no grind erases the first, and there is no pin or menu to manage.

Jack of All Trades (the breadth build)

If your two highest crafts are not adjacent you do not form an archetype: you become a Jack of All Trades (subtitle "master of none..."). It is a legitimate build, not a penalty. Where an archetype goes deep and narrow, a Jack goes broad and shallow.

  • Breadth ceiling: a Jack can level every craft to a mid tier (uncommon or rare, TBD) but none to legendary, so a Jack is the only crafter who can make decent versions of all ten lines, where a specialist is stuck at the common floor on seven of them.
  • Improviser's perks: a random chance to produce a better or worse version of what you craft, and reduced material cost from synergistic cross-craft knowledge.
  • No combos: signature combo recipes need an adjacent pair, so a Jack trades the exclusive high-tier lines for versatility.

Titles are earned, not default: a brand-new crafter has none, every title is granted by doing. But no one who has specialized is left in limbo. Once you reach the rare tier the game offers you a title to attune to, an archetype if your two highest crafts are adjacent, Jack of All Trades if not, so there is never a confusing "high crafts but no recognized identity" gap.

Tier distribution is TBD tuning against itemization. The working numbers: a specialist reaches legendary on the 2 majors, rare on the 1 hobby, and common on the other 7; depending on how items are tiered this may shift up to legendary, epic, and uncommon (2, 1, 7). A Jack reaches uncommon (or rare) across all ten, never legendary. The exact ceilings, and whether the hobby caps at rare or epic, are open. (Figure 3 above is an illustrative reachable envelope, not a specific spend.)

Battlefield Experience: production skill from observed item use (exploratory, later phase)

Production skill could also grow from Battlefield Experience, not just crafting: watching crafted items get used in combat grants a small trickle of specialization xp toward the craft that makes them, when that craft is part of your active archetype. A weapon that lands a killing blow or armor worn while a monster dies feeds xp to a Smith; a potion drunk or meal eaten feeds nothing unless alchemy or cooking is your specialty.

  • Only your active archetype. Battlefield Experience credits only the crafts in your current specialty (your two majors, and your hobby up to its cap). It never advances a craft outside your archetype, so witnessing a wide variety of items in combat cannot quietly make you better at everything. This also closes the alt loophole: each character earns Battlefield Experience only for its own active archetype, so holding several archetypes across alts never lets one account skirt the at-most-two-active rule.
  • Additive only, never a cost. Because you do not control what you witness, Battlefield Experience can only add; it never reduces another craft and never shifts your attunement. It is a separate channel that feeds your active specialty alongside deliberate crafting.
  • Uncommon or rare tier and above only (the exact floor is tuning), so it never touches the common free floor and only kicks in at the tiers where specialization actually matters.
  • Scope is a server-cost question, and a fairness question. Ideally anything within your view counts, with party and raid members counting for more, and observing strangers worth less and more distance-constrained than observing your own group. This maps onto the server's existing player and event interest scope (roughly 90 yards; the 120-yard radius is NPC-only). Whether 90 yards is enough, and whether group Battlefield Experience credit should reach farther than stranger credit, is a playtest question.
  • Consumables improve the recipe; equipment improves the item. Battlefield Experience splits on the consumable-versus-equipment axis. For a consumable, it accrues to the recipe, so witnessing your potions, food, or bombs used in combat makes every future craft of that recipe a little better (a healing potion that restores +10 more HP). For a piece of equipment, it accrues to the specific item, so after a dungeon run you can make improvements to that exact piece, effectively an extra armor-kit-style enhancement slot (the full power and effect menu is TBD). Any crafter can apply an equipment improvement, but the one who originally made it improves it most (like the original-crafter recharge discount), rewarding getting your gear from the friends you play with.
Worked example. A guild's master armorcrafter forges a set for a member, then comes along on the dungeon. Over a dungeon's worth of fights they observe that armor taking and surviving hits, and afterward they can take it back and improve it a little for its owner. This is why a guild may prefer to bring its master crafters on raids rather than just hauling materials back to a crafter who never leaves town.

An idea, not a committed mechanic. It needs careful tuning and would land much later, after the core systems are proven.

Crafted gear: quality and power

Recipes consume materials and require the relevant craft skill. Each crafted result rolls a quality on the game's existing item rarity tiers (poor, common, uncommon, rare, epic, legendary), which scales the stat roll within the item's item-level budget. Average quality rises with proficiency and material tier; individual rolls still vary.

Where crafted combat gear sits

Crafting is a genuine path to strong gear, not a strictly capped sideline. The position on the power curve scales with specialization concentration and material tier:

  • An average crafter (using lots of materials but not epic-tier) makes gear on par with rare to epic normal dungeon drops.
  • A peaked specialist using epic or better materials can make gear equal to, or in places better than, some dungeon drops.
  • Beyond raw item level, crafting carries unique effects available only through crafting (procs, utility, set-style bonuses) that give crafted gear its own identity.

Figure 4. Crafted power, two levers

Item-level budget (the horizontal band) overlaps with dungeon drops at the high end for specialists with epic materials, rather than being hard-capped below them. Crafting-only effects are an orthogonal axis: even where item level matches a drop, the crafted piece can do something a drop cannot.

Binding: gear crafted for a recipient

The game has no binding today, and crafted gear is where we introduce it, because the fantasy is that the piece was made for you. Binding is a per-recipe decision along one axis:

  • Low-tier gear is unbound. It crafts like any item and anyone can pick it up and use it, which preserves the common-floor promise that a level 1 can hammer out armor for a friend.
  • High-tier gear is bound to a recipient. Only the named character can equip it, but the item is still freely tradable. To keep this distinct from classic bind-on-pickup or bind-on-equip, the item's binding field is equipBoundTo (bound to a character for equipping, not for trading). Such a piece can go back to its crafter for enhancement, or off to a salvager once its owner is done with it; it simply cannot be worn by anyone but the person it was made for.

Bind-to-recipient does the interdependence work without flooding the auction house with top-tier gear: a specialist stays the real supplier, and demand routes to a named crafter rather than the cheapest anonymous listing. It also opens the lore the system is reaching for, a warrior claims a trophy from a beast (monster harvest, signed material) and brings it to a master crafter to be forged into their weapon or armor. Some required materials can be tied to the intended recipient, reinforcing that the piece is genuinely theirs (a later extension, since it needs order-time escrow).

Commissioning bound gear

Because a high-tier piece is made for a named recipient, it is produced through a commission workflow rather than crafted speculatively, and the recipient is the person who placed the order, so no one can mistype a name and brick an expensive item. There is a lot of room to iterate on the UX; the shape:

  • A player places an order for the item they want, scoped either open to any capable crafter, or narrowed to their guild, their friends list, or a specific crafter.
  • Orders can be canceled any time before a crafter accepts one.
  • Crafters browse an orders list with search and filters and pick up an order they want to fulfill.
  • The flow is deliberately multi-step (accept, craft, deliver), so a high-cost item is never lost to a single misclick, and the equipBoundTo recipient is set from the order, not typed by the crafter.

Specialization perks (beyond raw output)

Pushing far into one region of the wheel unlocks perks, not just a higher item-level ceiling. Examples (exact set is tuning):

  • Reduced resource usage: recipes in your specialized crafts cost fewer materials.
  • Mobile crafting station: set up a temporary station in the field, so you can craft while adventuring.
  • Crafter conveniences: cheaper effect recharges on tools you crafted, faster crafts, higher maximum quality, or specialist-only recipes.

Gathering professions (universal)

Everyone can perform all gathering, and gathering one thing must never reduce skill at gathering another (no conserved mass here). Proficiency per profession grows independently by use and raises the rarity of materials obtained; higher-rarity materials are the primary input that lets production reach higher output tiers. The higher-rarity nodes live out in the higher and more dangerous zones, not in a safe in-town loop, so the top of gathering has its own soft risk gate; depth curves with the zones. Starter set (extensible): Mining, Logging, Herbalism, Monster Harvesting.

World-spawn nodes (Mining / Logging / Herbalism)

  • Nodes are permanent fixtures of the world.
  • Harvest state respawns per player. Each player has their own respawn timer on a given node; one player harvesting it does not deny it to anyone else.
  • So there is no gather rush or node camping, and a tool's "faster respawn" bonus is meaningful to the individual who owns it.
  • Node density and dynamic / location-based respawn are future topics. The initial implementation uses static placements with a fixed per-player respawn timer.

Monster Harvesting (single-use corpses) and focus

The deliberate opposite of world nodes, to make specialization social and to justify higher-value materials.

  • Each corpse can be gathered once, first come. Corpses are a shared, scarce resource.
  • Scarcity rewards coordination: a regular group plans who specializes in which monster component types, so each member knows which monsters to harvest and which to leave for a teammate.
  • A monster is tagged with one or more component types (horn, hide, venom sac, gills, ...), defined per mob as content.
  • At the corpse, the UI lets the player focus on a single component type or several at once. Fewer components yields higher tier; spreading across many yields lower tier each.

Figure 5. Per-corpse extraction tradeoff

A drake corpse tagged with three component types. Concentrating extraction on one component returns a high tier; splitting across all three returns a lower tier of each. The persistent town focus shifts these tiers further up for your specialized components.

Focus: additive allocation (voluntary)

On top of the per-corpse choice, a player sets a persistent focus in town. Focus is additive: every component starts at a guaranteed baseline that is never penalized, and focus points add rarity/yield bonuses to chosen components only. The only cost is opportunity. This lets a group divide the work: each member focuses different components so the party covers more high-tier materials between them.

Figure 6. Additive focus: the baseline never drops

Monster Harvesting focus over component types. The inner solid ring is the guaranteed baseline for every component. Focus points add the outer band on chosen components only (here a venom specialist boosting venom sacs and glands). Unfocused components stay at baseline, never below. Contrast the production wheel, where only one specialty is empowered at a time rather than every axis at once.

Signed materials and the self-gathered bonus

  • Gathering a rare or better monster material stamps the item with the gatherer's name (a signed material).
  • Using a material you gathered yourself in a craft grants a bonus: either a small lift to the output's item-level budget, or a reduction in the quantity of that material the recipe requires.
  • This rewards the player who does both halves, and turns a signed material from someone else into a mark of provenance rather than a transferable mechanical bonus.

Why account-level breadth does not break exclusivity

An account can hold many characters, so a dedicated player can in principle spread the archetypes across alts and cover the whole wheel over time. That does not collapse the design, because the top of crafting is gated behind adventuring, not just crafting. The best materials come from Monster Harvesting, which means killing higher-tier monsters, and later Battlefield Experience (the channel above, still exploratory) improves what you craft. Both require a character that actually fights, so a combat level 1 crafting alt is never prevented from crafting but is capped well below an adventurer-crafter. Monster Harvesting alone carries this gate today; Battlefield Experience only deepens it. This is the pillar that negates the one-alt-per-craft pattern that guts crafting value in so many MMOs.

Two further inputs reinforce it. Contested single-use corpses are an always-on friction no one can multibox. And a recipe that requires a material signed by its recipient removes the shared-gatherer-alt shortcut for bound gear, because the wearer has to have gathered it themselves. A bare signature on its own is only provenance, not an anti-alt lever (each alt signs its own materials); the exclusivity comes from the adventuring gate and the contested and recipient-bound inputs, not the signature. You can only ever play one or two characters at once, so you can never bring the top of every archetype to bear at the same time. Specialization stays per-character.

Tools

A gathering tool is two layers: a durable base tool plus consumable effects slotted onto it.

Base tool: tier, infinite durability

  • Each base tool has a tier. Your gathering skill gates which tier you can use; the tool's tier in turn gates which nodes (and which monster materials) you can gather at all.
  • The base tool has infinite durability, so you are never stranded in the wild unable to gather. Basic base tools are sold by vendors; higher tiers are crafted.
  • A base tool also carries a rarity on the standard item tiers. Rarity does not change tier gating; it drives how often effects spend durability (see below).

Effects: durable, rechargeable bonuses

  • Effects are the bonuses (quantity, quality, respawn speed), slotted onto a base tool and produced by a production craft. Effects have their own durability and deplete with use; when an effect runs out the base tool keeps working at tier, just without that bonus.
  • Effects are rechargeable. Two ways: the production craft that created the effect, or an Enchanter / Scribe. Recharging through the original crafter of the tool is cheaper and faster.
  • The recharge cost is materials aligned with the craft used, so effects are a recurring materials sink.
  • Always or Prompt on Use: each high-value effect can be configured in the UI to fire Always or to Prompt on Use, so you do not burn a precious charge on a target you did not mean to spend it on.
  • Rarity-scaled consumption: the chance to spend a unit of effect durability depends on the tool's rarity versus the target's rarity. Against an equal-or-higher-rarity target the tool spends a charge every use; against lower-rarity targets it often spends nothing. Example: an epic Monster Scalpel with +3 quantity spends 1 durability on an epic-equivalent monster, roughly 60 percent on a rare-equivalent, roughly 10 percent on a common-equivalent.
Tuning guardrail: effect costs should be meaningful but never so high that they discourage use or cost more than the materials being gathered are worth. The rarity-scaled consumption is part of that, so a high-end tool sips charges on trivial targets and saves them for valuable ones.

Fragility: why the wheel resists new crafts

Load-bearing warning for future contributors: the production wheel is a tightly coupled structure. Do not casually add an eleventh craft. Read this section first.

The wheel's elegance is exactly what makes it brittle. Every craft's opposite (its hobby source and synergy) and its neighbors (its valid master-pairs) are fixed by position and count.

  • Adding one craft makes the count odd, which breaks clean diameters.
  • Adding two keeps parity but shifts every craft's opposite, re-deriving all archetypes, hobby maps, and synergies, and can invalidate existing characters. It is a versioned migration, not a tweak.
  • The mirror theorem. Because the ring is symmetric across its diameters, every archetype is welded to a mirror, the archetype made from the opposites of its two majors. The ten archetypes are really five mirror-pairs; you cannot keep one half and drop the other. With the current opposites the welds are: Smith and Outfitter; Cogsmith and Mageweaver; Bombardier and Arcanist; Apothecary and Gembinder; Trapper and Bladewright. This matters when curating the set for name quality and thematic fit (not gameplay power, every archetype is balanced to be viable): an archetype with a clumsy name or a strained pairing of its two crafts is often the mirror of one whose name and synergy you want to keep, so you cannot drop the awkward one without losing the good one too.

So the policy is to keep the wheel small and stable and route growth through two lanes that are not on the wheel:

  1. Gathering skills (off-wheel, additive, free): add as many as you like; the only decision is which craft consumes the output.
  2. Sub-disciplines and cosmetic/housing crafts (off-wheel or nested): specializations of an existing craft (Poisoncraft under Alchemy) or a separate non-combat system (see Future).

Only grow the wheel itself in deliberate, planned opposite pairs, and only for genuinely new combat-relevant gear/consumable domains. A 12-craft construction variant (Carpenter + Mason) was prototyped and works, but it forces two stretch archetypes and a full re-lay of the ring, so it is deferred (see Discarded and Future).

Architecture (how it lands in this repo)

The system follows the repo's existing seams. Nothing below invents a new one.

Determinism and authority (hard invariants)

  • All gathering and crafting logic lives in src/sim/ and obeys the sim invariants: no DOM/Three imports, all randomness through Rng, fixed 20 Hz tick, no Math.random / Date.now / performance.now. Per-player node respawn timers, corpse-harvest state, effect durability/recharge, mobile crafting stations, and re-spec timers advance on the sim clock.
  • The server is authoritative. Harvest rolls, quality rolls, recipe resolution, material consumption, the self-gathered bonus, signed-material stamping, effect-durability consumption rolls, effect recharge, focus re-spec, and skill changes resolve server-side. The client streams intent and renders results.

Content-as-code (src/sim/content/, merged by data.ts)

  • professions.ts: the ten-craft wheel (the ring order and the per-slot tier ceilings), the archetype names, craft outputs (permanent / consumable / weapon lines), gathering profession definitions, focus and component-type configuration, specialization perks, base tool tiers, and the effect catalog.
  • recipes.ts: recipe records (inputs, craft + skill requirement, output, base ilvl budget, crafting-only effects, per-recipe binding policy).
  • gather_nodes.ts: world-node definitions and placements; monster component-type tags live on mob records.
  • Attunement quests (the per-archetype lore quest and the repeatable make-amends switch quest) are quest content; new craft and gather quest objective types let quests credit crafting and gathering.
  • Fungible materials and base tools are item records, so they flow through the existing inventory, vendor, and market systems unchanged.

Per-item-instance model (a foundation, built early)

Inventory today is itemId + count and items carry no identity. Signed materials, charged tools, rolled-quality crafted gear, and bind-to-recipient gear all need per-item identity: an instance id plus a payload (gatherer signature, per-effect charges, rolled quality/stats, the equipBoundTo recipient). This is cross-cutting (inventory, equipment, trade, market, vendor, the interest-scoped snapshot, the wire encoder, and JSONB load/save) and is spec'd as its own early slice before tools, monster harvesting, and crafted gear. Fungible stacks (raw materials, infinite-durability base tools) keep flowing as itemId + count and do not need it.

New sim system behind the SimContext seam (src/sim/professions/)

  • gathering.ts: node harvest, per-player respawn state, corpse harvest, proficiency gain and rarity rolls.
  • crafting.ts: recipe resolution, material consumption, quality roll, ilvl budget computation, crafting-only effect application.
  • wheel.ts: the conserved-mass mastery model (additive skill gain, the at-most-two active cap, the active archetype and attunement, the adjacent-pair majors and capped opposite hobby, specialization-concentration readout). Attunement switching is quest-driven (see Production core), not decay.
  • focus.ts: the additive gathering focus allocation plus the time/coin/material re-spec.
  • tools.ts: base-tool tier gating plus effect application, depletion, and recharge.

Per the seam rules, these modules hold functions; backing state (per-player craft skills, the active archetype and attunement history, the current hobby, focus allocations, respawn timers, corpse flags, re-spec timers) lives on Sim as live ctx views, with thin same-named delegates where a foreign caller resolves a method.

The IWorld seam, client, persistence

  • IWorld (src/world_api.ts): extend first, implement in both Sim and ClientWorld, then consume from render/ui. New surface: read craft skills and the wheel, current majors/hobby, specialization perks, focus allocation, node availability, corpse component options, recipe lists and craft results, tool tier and effect charges.
  • Client (render / ui): a professions/wheel HUD window following the module-first UI rules. All player-visible strings are t() keys; numbers/money through the formatters. Sim/server emit stable keys plus values re-localized by the matcher.
  • Guide / wiki: player-facing professions, gathering, and recipe content feeds /wiki; add guide.* prose keys and run npm run wiki:content.
  • Persistence (server/): per-player craft skills, the active archetype, attunement history (which archetypes have been entered and the make-amends count per archetype), current hobby, focus allocations, and learned recipes persist (JSONB on the character) with additive back-compat load. Signed-material gatherer names, per-effect charges, rolled quality/stats, and the equipBoundTo recipient persist on item instances. Respawn timers and mobile stations are sim runtime state.

Build order (slices)

Each slice is its own spec, plan, and implementation cycle.

  1. Gathering foundation (suggested first slice): universal gathering skills, proficiency to material rarity, materials as items, vendor tools, world-node spawn plus per-player harvest/respawn. Kept fully fungible, so it does not need the item-instance model. No wheel yet. Note: shipping gathering well ahead of crafting lets players stockpile raw materials and speed-level the early crafting tiers when crafting lands. The later tiers gate on signed materials and Battlefield Experience that cannot be stockpiled, so this is a feel concern, not a balance break. Two cheap mitigations that also fit the depth-curves-with-the-zones principle: keep zone-1 gathering to common and low-tier materials (rare and up nodes ship later with their higher zones and their crafting), and ship a handful of common recipes or a vendor sink alongside this slice so materials have an immediate use rather than piling up.
  2. Per-item-instance foundation: an instance id plus payload (signer, charges, rolled quality/stats, the equipBoundTo recipient) across inventory, equipment, trade, market, vendor, the snapshot, the wire, and persistence. Cross-cutting, and it must land before tools, harvesting, and crafted gear, which all carry item identity. Note: only higher-tier crafts carry item identity, so common-tier crafting can ship fungibly before this slice; the instance model gates the high-tier work, not the first playable crafting.
  3. Production core: the ten crafts and their outputs, recipes, crafting, the conserved-mass wheel (additive skill, the at-most-two active cap, adjacent-pair majors, capped opposite hobby), attunement (the per-tier quest ladder: the first-time lore quest and the repeatable make-amends switch quest), tiered mastery (the per-tier quest gate, xp lost above the current ceiling, diminishing returns below capability), combo-specific signature recipes, rarity-tier quality, ilvl-budget plus crafting-only effects, bind-to-recipient (equipBoundTo) on high-tier gear with its commission/order workflow, and the first specialization perks. Start with low-stakes consumables, then enhancements, then gear.
  4. Crafted tools: base tool tiers (infinite durability) plus slotted effects with durability and craft-aligned recharge.
  5. Monster Harvesting plus focus: single-use corpses, component-type tags, per-corpse focus picker, the additive town focus, the time/coin/material re-spec, and signed rare+ materials with the self-gathered craft bonus.
  6. Cosmetics / market integration, wiki polish, balance.

Much later, exploratory: Battlefield Experience (observed item use); and a possible off-wheel cosmetic/housing system (Carpenter, Mason, and tending skills) if the ten feel too limited.

Open questions / TBD

  • Production wheel: confirm the exact ring order, the per-slot tier ceilings (majors, hobby, others) that realize the at-most-two-adjacent rule, and the attunement cost curve.
  • Tiered mastery: crafts-per-tier counts (the example uses about 20), the diminishing-returns curve below capability (zero at a two-tier delta), and which recipes each tier unlocks. Decided: the at-most-two cap applies at uncommon and up, common is a free floor.
  • Hobby cap: confirm the ceiling tier (rare, possibly epic per itemization). Switching is decided: the hobby switches as part of re-attuning, with no skill loss.
  • Tier distribution (itemization tuning): a specialist's working spend is 2 majors legendary, 1 hobby rare, 7 crafts common, which may shift to legendary / epic / uncommon (2 / 1 / 7). The Jack of All Trades ceiling (uncommon vs rare across all ten) and whether it gets a higher combined ceiling to afford that breadth are open.
  • Attunement quests: the design of the per-tier quest ladder (the first-time lore quest and each deeper tier quest) and the repeatable make-amends switch quest, and how the make-amends cost escalates with repeat switches (time, coin, materials, lore) while staying cheap enough early to let a player try a couple of archetypes; whether the hobby's cap is a hard cap. Decided: escalation is tuning, carried through quest flavor text.
  • Leveling and the tier ladder. Decided: crafts climb through a per-tier quest ladder; crafting above your current tier ceiling loses the excess xp; the ceiling is set either by attunement (a craft outside your active archetype) or by an incomplete tier quest, both identically; and learned skill is retained and re-empowered on re-attune, never re-earned. Open: the exact tier-quest designs, and whether a non-active craft still produces at the floor while capped.
  • Jack of All Trades perks: the magnitude of the better/worse output variance and the material-saving bonus.
  • Attunement legibility: the in-craft trend nudges, and the wording of the attunement quest's what-changes summary.
  • Binding: the per-recipe bind-to-recipient policy (which output classes bind via equipBoundTo, which tier threshold triggers it, and which required materials tie to the recipient). Decided: the recipient is set from the commission order, not typed by the crafter. Open: the commission/order UX (order scope to open, guild, friends, or a specific crafter; cancel-before-accept; the crafter orders list; the multi-step accept, craft, deliver flow).
  • Per-item-instance model: instance id + payload (signer, charges, rolled stats, the equipBoundTo recipient); how two rolled crafted items stack, list, and persist; and its slice sequencing before the higher-tier crafts (tools, harvesting, crafted gear), which is what carries item identity.
  • Economy: an output throttle for a maxed specialist, and a real gold sink in proportion to the new material faucet (the 5 percent market cut is the only sink today).
  • Alts. Decided: specialization stays per-character, and exclusivity is anchored by the adventuring gate (the crafting ceiling needs Monster Harvesting materials and Battlefield Experience, which cap a combat level 1 crafting alt below an adventurer-crafter), backed by contested corpses and recipient-signed materials, not by character slots. A fully rewarding pure-skiller route is an explicit non-goal for now; revisit only with its own brainstorm.
  • Gathering-first sequencing: whether to accept material stockpiling before crafting ships, or add a soft mitigation.
  • Battlefield Experience (exploratory): separate channel scoped to the active archetype only (no skirting the at-most-two rule via breadth or alts), observation radius (the 90-yard player scope, and whether group credit should reach farther than stranger credit), per-item attribution, and the tier floor (uncommon or rare and above) tuning.
  • Battlefield Experience and Jack of All Trades: whether a Jack (who has no active archetype) benefits from Battlefield Experience at all, and if so for which crafts (all ten, none, or only their highest few), so the active-archetype gate has a defined answer for the breadth build.
  • Gathering focus: skill tree vs additive radar, focus granularity, and the re-spec cost curves.
  • Output quality and ilvl budget: how the rarity-tier roll maps to stat rolls, and the exact curve over concentration, material tier, and skill.
  • Crafting-only effects: the design space and how they are balanced against item level.
  • Specialization perks: the exact perk set and the thresholds that unlock them.
  • Monster-tier to item-tier mapping: the shared rarity scale that the rarity-scaled effect consumption and tool gating read from.
  • Tool effects: the effect catalog, depletion rates, the rarity-scaled consumption curve, and recharge material costs (base tool durability is decided: infinite).
  • Signed materials: confirm rare+ monster materials sign, and the size of the self-gathered craft bonus.
  • Node density and dynamic / location-based respawn (explicitly future); persistence scope for respawn state across restarts.
  • Whether and when to build the off-wheel cosmetic/housing system.
